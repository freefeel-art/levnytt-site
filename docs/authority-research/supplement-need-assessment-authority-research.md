# Behöver jag kosttillskott? — Authority Research Library

**Topic:** Behöver jag kosttillskott? När räcker maten och vem behöver verkligen extra vitaminer och mineraler?
**Language:** Swedish
**Date:** 2026-06-30
**Research confidence:** High
**Scope:** This document summarizes the current scientific and regulatory authority landscape for determining who needs dietary supplements. It addresses the fundamental consumer question: "Do I need supplements or is food enough?" — a decision-making framework based on official recommendations, population dietary data, at-risk groups, and evidence on the benefits (and lack thereof) of supplementation in well-nourished populations. This is a research input, not a finished article.

---

## 1. Topic

The question "Behöver jag kosttillskott?" is the most fundamental consumer decision in the supplement market. Official guidance from Livsmedelsverket (Swedish Food Agency) states that most people get enough vitamins and minerals from food. The Nordic Nutrition Recommendations 2023 conclude that for healthy individuals eating a varied, energy-sufficient diet, long-term nutrient intake from supplements has not been shown to be health-promoting. Yet contradictory signals — population-wide micronutrient inadequacies, specific at-risk group recommendations, and the booming supplement market — create consumer confusion.

This entry covers: the official positions of Livsmedelsverket and NNR2023, at-risk population groups, evidence for (and against) population-wide supplementation, dietary intake data relevant to Swedish conditions, the limitations of the "food first" message, and the ethical/communication challenge of distinguishing genuine need from unnecessary use.

The challenge lies in communicating this nuance without alienating consumers who may benefit while discouraging unnecessary use.

## 3. Official positions from authorities

### 3.1 Livsmedelsverket (Swedish Food Agency)

- **Primary page — Kosttillskott:** "De allra flesta får i sig tillräckligt med vitaminer och mineraler med vanlig mat. Men vissa grupper behöver kosttillskott."
  - Type: Official position
  - Importance: Foundational message. Establishes "food first" as default, with targeted exceptions.

- **Vem behöver extra vitaminer och mineraler?**
  - Specific groups identified:
    - Alla barn upp till två års ålder (vitamin D)
    - Barn och vuxna som inte äter fisk och D-vitaminberikade livsmedel
    - Barn och vuxna som bär kläder som täcker ansikte, armar och ben eller inte är ute i solen under sommaren
    - Alla äldre över 75 år (vitamin D)
    - Kvinnor som planerar graviditet (folsyra)
    - Personer som utesluter hela livsmedelsgrupper (t.ex. veganer — B12)
  - Type: Official recommendation
  - Importance: Primary Swedish reference for who truly needs supplements.

- **Key statement on unnecessary use:** "Trots att det finns många brister i våra matvanor visar kostundersökningar att de allra flesta får i sig tillräckligt med vitaminer och mineraler genom den vanliga maten."
  - Type: Official position
  - Importance: Acknowledges dietary quality issues but distinguishes them from micronutrient deficiency.

### 3.2 Nordic Nutrition Recommendations 2023 (NNR2023)

- **Kosttillskott section:** "Bland friska personer som äter en varierad och energimässigt tillräcklig kost har långvarigt intag av näringsämnen från kosttillskott generellt inte visats vara hälsofrämjande eller kunnat kopplas till minskad risk för sjukdomar relaterade till levnadsvanor."
  - Type: Evidence-based guideline
  - Importance: Highest Nordic scientific authority. Directly states lack of preventive benefit.

- **Harm signal:** "Tvärtom finns det evidens för att högt intag av vissa kosttillskott, framför allt vitaminer med antioxidativa egenskaper, skulle kunna leda till negativa hälsoeffekter såsom ökad risk för förtida död."
  - Type: Evidence summary
  - Importance: Balances the "no benefit" message with a "potential harm" warning.

- **Conclusion on supplementation:** "Därmed finns det ingen vetenskapligt underbyggd anledning att låta kosttillskott kompensera en obalanserad kost."
  - Type: Guideline recommendation
  - Importance: Critical for LevNytt's messaging. Supplements are not a shortcut.

- **Energy threshold:** "I NNR står det att det kan vara svårt att täcka behovet av vitaminer och mineraler om energiintaget är lägre än 1500 kcal per dag."
  - Type: Guideline
  - Importance: Practical threshold for clinicians and consumers.

### 3.3 Livsmedelsverket — NNR2023 implementation for Sweden

- Vitamin D: "Äldre personer över 60 år med liten eller ingen solexposition, samt alla över 75 år rekommenderas ett intag på 20 μg vitamin D dagligen, vilket kräver tillskott utöver kosten." (Läkemedelsboken / NNR 2023 implementation)
- Folic acid: "Kvinnor som önskar bli gravida rekommenderas ett intag av 400 μg folat per dag." (Läkemedelsboken)
- Importance: Concrete, actionable thresholds for specific at-risk groups.

### 3.4 WHO (World Health Organization)

- **Global perspective:** WHO estimates >2 billion people worldwide are deficient in key micronutrients (vitamin A, iodine, iron, zinc). Targeted supplementation, food fortification, and dietary diversification are recommended strategies.
- **Type:** Global health guidance
- **Importance:** Establishes that supplementation is a legitimate public health intervention for deficiency contexts — but this applies primarily to low- and middle-income countries, not Sweden.

### 3.5 Läkemedelsboken (Swedish Medicines Book)

- **Multivitamins:** "Multivitaminpreparat med mineraler i doser som inte överstiger NNR 2023 kan vara indicerat vid generell malnutrition med lågt intag av såväl energigivande näringsämnen som vitaminer, mineraler och spårämnen."
  - Type: Clinical guidance
  - Importance: Defines the clinical boundary where supplementation is appropriate — diagnosed/general malnutrition under medical supervision.

## 5. Major systematic reviews and key studies

### 5.1 Benefit of supplements for general population

- **NNR 2023 systematic review process (multiple background papers, 2020–2023).** Comprehensive evaluation of evidence linking supplement use to chronic disease prevention in well-nourished populations.
  - Finding: No consistent evidence that multivitamin/mineral supplements reduce risk of cardiovascular disease, cancer, or all-cause mortality in well-nourished adults. Some evidence of harm from high-dose single-nutrient supplements.
  - Importance: Foundational for the "food first" message.

- **Bjelakovic, G. et al. (2012).** *Antioxidant supplements for prevention of mortality in healthy participants and patients with various diseases.* Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews.
  - Finding: Meta-analysis of 78 RCTs (296,707 participants). Beta-carotene, vitamin E, and higher doses of vitamin A were associated with increased all-cause mortality.
  - Importance: Gold-standard Cochrane review demonstrating potential harm from antioxidant supplements.

- **Jenkins, D.J.A. et al. (2018).** *Supplemental Vitamins and Minerals for CVD Prevention and Treatment.* Journal of the American College of Cardiology. Evidence-based position paper.
  - Finding: Most vitamin/mineral supplements do not prevent CVD. Folic acid (for stroke reduction in regions without fortification) and B vitamin combinations showed some benefit in specific contexts.
  - Importance: Comprehensive review showing limited preventive benefit.

### 5.2 Global micronutrient inadequacy

- **Simmons, C. et al. (2024).** *Global estimation of dietary micronutrient inadequacies: a modelling analysis.* The Lancet (pre-print / published, cited in PubMed as PMID 39218000).
  - Finding: >5 billion people consume inadequate iodine (68%), vitamin E (67%), and calcium (66%). >4 billion inadequate iron (65%), riboflavin (55%), folate (54%), and vitamin C (53%). Women had higher inadequacy for iodine, B12, iron, selenium. Men higher for magnesium, B6, zinc, vitamin C, vitamin A, thiamin, niacin.
  - Importance: First global estimates using dietary intake data, not biomarker or food supply proxies. Contextualizes Swedish adequacy against global patterns.

- **WHO (multiple reports).** Vitamin A, iron, iodine, and zinc deficiencies identified as the most widespread micronutrient deficiencies globally, particularly affecting children and pregnant women in low- and middle-income countries.
  - Importance: Establishes the global public health rationale for supplementation programs.

### 5.3 Swedish dietary intake data

- **Riksmaten vuxna 2010–11** (Livsmedelsverket). Swedish national dietary survey of adults 18–80 years.
  - Key findings relevant to supplement need:
    - Vitamin D: Median intake from food only ~5–6 μg/day. Well below the 10 μg/day RDI. Supplement users had substantially better status.
    - Folate: Median intake below recommended levels for women of childbearing age.
    - Iron: Median intake adequate for men but insufficient for many menstruating women.
    - Iodine: Intake has declined with reduced dairy consumption and iodised salt use is not universal.
    - Selenium: Median intake ~35–40 μg/day, below the RDI of ~55–75 μg/day.
  - Importance: Best available data on Swedish population intake. Supports targeted supplementation for specific nutrients.

- **Riksmaten ungdom 2016–17.** Swedish national dietary survey of adolescents.
  - Key findings: Lower intake of vitamin D, folate, iron, and iodine compared to recommendations among subgroups.
  - Importance: Supplements indication for specific adolescent subgroups.

### 5.4 Nordic-specific nutrient status

- **Vitamin D:** Nordic countries have a unique challenge due to limited UVB exposure during winter months (October–March). Serum 25(OH)D levels drop significantly in winter. Livsmedelsverket and NNR2023 recommend vitamin D supplementation for:
  - All infants (from 1 week to 2 years)
  - All adults over 75 years (20 μg/day)
  - Adults over 60 with minimal sun exposure
  - Anyone who covers their skin or avoids sun during summer
  - Pregnant and lactating women

- **Iodine:** Sweden has declining iodine intake due to reduced fish, dairy, and egg consumption. No mandatory salt iodisation in Sweden (unlike Norway, Finland, Denmark).
  - Importance: Emerging concern. Iodine deficiency may re-emerge as a public health issue in Sweden.

- **Folate:** NNR2023 and Livsmedelsverket recommend folic acid supplementation (400 μg/day) for women planning pregnancy and through week 12 of gestation. No mandatory flour fortification in Sweden (unlike many other countries).

### 5.5 At-risk group evidence

- **Vegans and vegetarians:** Well-documented risk of B12 deficiency. Deficiency can cause irreversible neurological damage. Supplementation is essential for strict vegans. NNR2023 and Livsmedelsverket clearly state this.
- **Elderly (75+):** Reduced skin synthesis of vitamin D, reduced dietary intake, polypharmacy interactions. Livsmedelsverket recommends 20 μg/day vitamin D supplement.
- **Pregnant women:** Increased requirements for folate, iron, vitamin D, and iodine. NNR2023 makes specific recommendations.
- **Low energy intake (<1500 kcal/day):** NNR2023 states it is difficult to meet micronutrient needs below this threshold. Relevant for weight loss, eating disorders, elderly, and frail individuals.
- **Malabsorption conditions:** Coeliac disease, Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis, gastric bypass — clinically indicated supplementation under medical supervision.

## 7. Established scientific consensus

1. **For a healthy adult eating a varied, energy-sufficient diet: food is sufficient.** NNR2023 and Livsmedelsverket are unambiguous on this point. Routine multivitamin use in this population provides no proven health benefit.
2. **Specific populations have evidence-based indications for supplementation.**
   - Infants: vitamin D (from 1 week to 2 years)
   - Elderly 75+: vitamin D (20 μg/day)
   - Pregnancy planning and first trimester: folic acid (400 μg/day)
   - Vegans: vitamin B12 (mandatory)
   - Limited sun exposure: vitamin D (dose depends on degree of limitation)
   - Diagnosed deficiency: targeted supplementation under medical guidance
3. **Food provides benefits that supplements cannot replicate.** Whole foods contain matrices of nutrients, fibre, and bioactive compounds. Supplementing isolated nutrients does not reproduce the health benefits of a healthy diet.
4. **The "food first, supplement second" framework** is the consensus position across all major health authorities in Sweden and the Nordic region.
5. **Supplements have a legitimate role** in public health for addressing documented deficiencies, supporting specific life stages, and managing certain medical conditions — but this role is targeted, not universal.
6. **Self-diagnosis and unnecessary supplementation are common.** The low barrier to purchase and the perception that supplements are "health insurance" drive widespread use without evidence of need.

## 8. Areas where evidence is mixed or uncertain

1. **Subclinical deficiencies.** The boundary between "adequate" and "inadequate" status is not always clear. Subclinical deficiencies may affect wellbeing without causing classical deficiency symptoms. Testing is imperfect and not routinely available.
2. **Individual variability in nutrient requirements.** Genetic polymorphisms (MTHFR, VDR, FUT2) affect nutrient metabolism and requirements for some individuals. Personalised supplementation is an emerging field but not yet supported by population-level guidelines.
3. **Optimal vs adequate intake.** NNR2023 RIs are set to cover 97.5% of the population. Optimal intake for long-term health may differ from the minimum to prevent deficiency, but evidence for higher targets is inconsistent.
4. **Role of supplementation in "modern dietary patterns."** Swedish dietary patterns have shifted — reduced fish, dairy, and whole grains; increased processed food. Whether these changes create new supplementation needs is debated.
5. **Long-term health effects of moderate-dose, long-duration multivitamin use.** The evidence is largely neutral (no benefit, no harm) but long-term RCT data is limited.
6. **Interaction between baseline nutrient status and supplement benefit.** Growing evidence suggests that supplement benefit (or harm) may depend on baseline status. The VITAL trial suggested possible benefit for those with lowest baseline omega-3, and the general pattern may apply to other nutrients.

## 9. Common misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction / nuance |
|---|---|
| "Alla behöver multivitamin för att vara säkra." | NNR 2023: inget vetenskapligt stöd för rutinmässigt multivitaminintag hos friska med varierad kost. |
| "Kosttillskott kan kompensera för dålig kost." | NNR 2023: "Ingen vetenskapligt underbyggd anledning." Matens helhetseffekter kan inte ersättas av isolerade näringsämnen. |
| "Om lite är bra, är mer bättre." | Falskt. Höga doser antioxidanter har i studier visat ökad risk. Guldkant: ATBC, SELECT, Cochrane. |
| "Man märker om man har brist." | Subklinisk brist ger ofta diffusa eller inga symptom. Trötthet, håravfall, nedsatt immunförsvar kan ha många orsaker. |
| "Svenskar har inga brister." | Riksmaten visar intagsgap för vitamin D, selen, jod, järn (kvinnor), och folat (fertila kvinnor). Brist är inte samma som otillräckligt intag — men gap finns. |
| "Man behöver bara äta hälsosamt så är allt täckt." | Även hälsosamma dieter kan ha gap i specifika näringsämnen, särskilt vitamin D vintertid, selen, jod och B12 vid växtbaserad kost. |
| "Blodprov kan alltid avgöra om du behöver tillskott." | Blodprov har begränsningar: vissa vitaminer (A, B12) reflekterar inte alltid vävnadsstatus. Referensintervall är inte alltid optimala nivåer. |
| "Barn behöver inga tillskott." | Alla barn 1 vecka–2 år rekommenderas D-vitamin. Barn med begränsad solexposition eller som utesluter livsmedelsgrupper kan behöva mer. |

## Factual consistency notes

- Livsmedelsverket states "de allra flesta får i sig tillräckligt" — this refers to the general population average, not to every individual. The caveat is important.
- NNR 2023's "ökad risk för förtida död" claim refers specifically to high-dose antioxidant supplements (beta-carotene, vitamin E, vitamin A), not to standard-dose multivitamins or all supplements generally.
- The 1500 kcal/day threshold from NNR2023 is a guideline, not a precise cutoff. Individual requirements vary.
- Riksmaten intake data shows average intakes, which can mask significant subgroup variation. Reported median intakes may not reflect adequacy for all.
- Global inadequacy data (Simmons 2024) estimates intake from food alone (excluding fortification and supplements). Actual deficiency prevalence is lower, especially in countries with fortification programs. Sweden has limited mandatory fortification.
- "Food first" does not mean "food only" — the consensus position explicitly acknowledges legitimate need for supplements in specific circumstances.

## Source labelling convention for downstream articles

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Fact** | Directly supported by primary data or official guideline. |
| **Guideline** | Official recommendation (Livsmedelsverket, NNR, EFSA, WHO). |

| **RCT evidence** | Supported by randomised controlled trial(s). |
