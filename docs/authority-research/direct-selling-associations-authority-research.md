## 1. Topic

**Direct selling associations (DSAs)** are trade organisations that represent direct-selling companies, promote the industry to policymakers and the public, publish ethics codes, collect industry statistics, and sometimes administer self-regulation. The largest are the **World Federation of Direct Selling Associations (WFDSA)**, the **European Direct Selling Association (Seldia)**, and national bodies such as the **Direct Selling Association (DSA) in the United States** and equivalent organisations in other countries.

### Core definitions

| Term | Definition | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **WFDSA (World Federation of Direct Selling Associations)** | Global umbrella organisation of national DSAs. | Publishes annual Global STATS report and a Code of Ethics. |
| **Seldia (formerly FEDSA)** | European direct selling trade association. | Lobbies EU institutions and promotes industry self-regulation. |
| **DSA-US / DSA-UK / national DSAs** | National direct-selling trade bodies. | Membership, ethics enforcement, and advocacy at national level. |
| **Code of Ethics** | Industry code setting standards for member companies. | Typically covers buy-back policies, earnings claims, and honest marketing. |
| **Self-regulation** | Industry-led enforcement of codes. | Complements but does not replace government regulation. |
| **Industry-funded research** | Statistics and studies funded or published by trade associations. | Useful for scale; subject to industry bias. |

## 5. Major research, reviews and regulatory decisions

### WFDSA

- Founded in 1978.
- Represents national DSAs in over 60 countries.
- Publishes annual Global STATS report.
- Code of Ethics covers:
  - Honest and accurate product claims
  - Accurate earnings representations
  - Buy-back or repurchase policies
  - Protection of salespersons
  - Respect for consumers

### Seldia

- European-level trade association.
- Engages with EU institutions on consumer-protection and digital-policy issues.
- Publishes industry positions on EU directives affecting direct selling.

### National DSAs

- **DSA-US:** Requires member companies to adhere to a Code of Ethics; has a self-regulatory council; provides DSA Direct Selling Self-Regulatory Council (DSSRC) for non-member companies as well.
- **DSSRC (Direct Selling Self-Regulatory Council):** Administered by BBB National Programs; reviews advertising and earnings claims. Decisions are advisory.
- Other national DSAs vary in size, activity, and enforcement transparency.

### WFDSA 2024 Global STATS highlights

- Global retail sales: ~$163.9 billion.
- Independent contractors: ~104.3 million.
- Female participation: ~72.1%.
- Top regions: Asia-Pacific, Americas, Europe.
- Top sectors: wellness, cosmetics/personal care, household goods/durables.

**Limitations:** Self-reported, not independently audited, mixes retail customers with business participants, does not report distributor profitability.

### Criticism and controversy

- **Self-regulation enforcement concerns:** Critics argue DSAs rarely expel members even when companies face regulatory action.
- **Industry-funded research:** Groß & Keep (2025) found that much academic literature is industry-tied and downplays risks.
- **Political lobbying:** DSAs actively lobby against stricter regulation; this is legal but can create policy capture concerns.
- **Blurring of retail customer and distributor categories:** Industry statistics may count people who joined mainly for product discounts as "independent contractors," inflating participation numbers.
- **Codes vs. reality:** A code of ethics exists, but compliance and sanctions are not always public or consistent.

## 7. Established scientific / regulatory consensus

1. **Direct selling associations are industry trade bodies, not government regulators.** They represent company interests and promote self-regulation.
2. **WFDSA is the primary global source of industry-wide statistics**, but its figures are self-reported and not independently audited.
3. **DSA codes of ethics set minimum standards for member companies**, but enforcement is self-regulatory and less transparent than government enforcement.
4. **Industry-funded research and advocacy should be treated with appropriate scepticism.** Independent peer-reviewed research has documented industry-tie bias.
5. **DSA membership is not a government endorsement or guarantee of profitability.** Consumers should not rely on membership alone when evaluating an opportunity.
6. **DSAs play a significant lobbying role** in the U.S., EU, and national capitals, often opposing stricter regulation.
7. **Self-regulation can complement but not replace government consumer-protection enforcement.**
8. **Headline industry statistics conflate retail customers, discount buyers, and business participants**, making them unsuitable for estimating distributor earnings.
9. **The Direct Selling Self-Regulatory Council (DSSRC) in the U.S. reviews advertising claims**, but its decisions are advisory, not legally binding.
10. **Consumers evaluating a direct-selling opportunity should verify claims independently, read income disclosures, and check government sources rather than relying on DSA membership.**

---

## 8. Areas of controversy

1. **How effective is DSA self-regulation?** Limited public evidence of sanctions or expulsions.
2. **Should DSAs be more transparent about member enforcement?** Critics argue consumers cannot assess code effectiveness without public records.
3. **Industry statistics reliability.** Self-reported data and mixed categories raise questions about accuracy.
4. **Lobbying influence.** DSAs oppose stricter rules; consumer advocates argue this weakens consumer protection.
5. **Relationship with regulators.** DSAs often cooperate with regulators but may also challenge enforcement actions.
6. **Code interpretation.** What counts as an "earnings claim" or "inventory loading" can be disputed.
7. **Digital transformation.** Traditional DSA models may not fully address social-media and app-based recruitment.
8. **Swedish/European DSA presence.** The strength and activity of national DSAs vary across Europe.

## 9. Common misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction / nuance |
|---|---|
| "DSA membership means a company is safe and ethical." | Membership indicates adherence to an industry code, not government certification or guarantee of profitability. |
| "WFDSA statistics prove MLM is profitable for most participants." | Statistics describe sales and participation, not distributor earnings. |
| "DSAs are government agencies." | DSAs are private trade associations. |
| "A code of ethics guarantees compliance." | Codes depend on self-enforcement; effectiveness varies. |
| "All direct-selling companies are DSA members." | Membership is voluntary; many companies are not members. |
| "Self-regulation makes government regulation unnecessary." | Self-regulation complements but does not replace legal enforcement. |
| "WFDSA data is independently audited." | WFDSA statistics are self-reported by member DSAs and companies. |

## Factual consistency notes

- **DSAs are trade associations, not regulators.** Do not present them as government agencies.
- **WFDSA statistics are self-reported.** Caveat all figures with source limitations.
- **Codes of ethics are voluntary self-regulation.** Compliance is not equivalent to legal compliance.
- **DSA membership is not a consumer guarantee.** Consumers should still read disclosures and check regulators.
- **DSSRC decisions are advisory, not legally binding.**
- **Distinguish between global (WFDSA), regional (Seldia), and national (DSA) bodies.**
- **Swedish DSA status should be verified before making strong claims about Swedish industry self-regulation.**
