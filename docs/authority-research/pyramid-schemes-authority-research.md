## 1. Topic

A **pyramid scheme** is an illegal business model in which participants earn money primarily from recruiting new participants, either through up-front fees or by purchasing inventory that must be bought to participate. The defining feature is that rewards flow from recruitment rather than from genuine sales to end users.

### Core definitions

| Term | Definition | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Pyramid scheme** | Illegal model where income derives mainly from recruiting other participants, not from retail sales. | Often collapses when recruitment slows. |
| **Ponzi scheme** | Fraud in which returns to earlier investors are paid from new investors' contributions; distinct from pyramid recruitment. | Both are illegal but structurally different. |
| **Product-based pyramid scheme** | A pyramid that uses product sales as a disguise; earnings still derive chiefly from recruitment and inventory loading. | Key modern regulatory challenge. |
| **Inventory loading** | Requirement or strong pressure to buy products in order to qualify for commissions or advance in rank. | One of the classic warning signs. |
| **Recruitment-driven compensation** | Commission structure that pays more for recruiting than for retail sales. | Central legal test in many jurisdictions. |
| **Ultimate user / retail customer** | A consumer who is not a participant in the business opportunity and buys products for personal use. | Sales to ultimate users support legitimacy. |

## 3. Tier 0 original sources

### Regulatory / legal / government

- **15 U.S. Code § 68 (Pyramid Promotional Schemes) — applicable depending on interpretation.** While the U.S. lacks a single federal criminal pyramid statute, pyramid schemes are prosecuted via FTC Act § 5, securities law, mail/wire fraud, and state laws.
  - **Type:** Statutory framework
  - **Importance:** Clarifies that FTC Act (15 U.S.C. § 45) prohibition of unfair/deceptive acts is the primary federal enforcement tool.

- **FTC v. Amway Corp. (1979).**
  - **Type:** Administrative decision
  - **Importance:** Established the product-sales test and "Amway safeguards" that distinguish legal MLM from pyramid schemes.

- **FTC v. BurnLounge, Inc. (9th Cir. 2015).**
  - **Type:** Federal appellate decision
  - **Importance:** Affirmed that BurnLounge was a pyramid scheme; clarified that the critical question is whether participants pay for the right to receive rewards primarily from recruitment rather than sales to ultimate users.

- **FTC / Herbalife settlement (2016).**
  - **Type:** Administrative settlement
  - **Importance:** Demonstrated that a product-based MLM can be found to have operated as a pyramid scheme if compensation is recruitment-driven; required $200 million redress and restructuring.

- **SEC v. W.J. Howey Co. (1946).** *Howey Test.*
  - **Type:** U.S. Supreme Court decision
  - **Importance:** Defines investment contract; used to determine when pyramid or MLM opportunities may constitute securities.

- **Directive 2005/29/EC.** *Unfair Commercial Practices Directive.*
  - **Type:** EU legislation
  - **Importance:** Prohibits misleading and aggressive practices that pyramid schemes typically use.

- **Marknadsföringslagen (2008:486).**
  - **Type:** Swedish legislation
  - **Importance:** Prohibits misleading marketing and can be used against pyramid-scheme promotion in Sweden.

- **Konsumentverket guidance on pyramid schemes.**
  - **Type:** Consumer-protection guidance
  - **Importance:** Swedish-language practical identification guidance.

### Independent research / advocacy

- **Consumer Awareness Institute / Jon Taylor. *The Case (For and) Against Multi-level Marketing.***
  - **Type:** Independent research report
  - **Importance:** Extensive critical compilation of MLM income data and pyramid-scheme analysis; advocacy-based but uses substantial primary data.

### Peer-reviewed research

- **Groß & Keep (2025).** *Review of direct selling / MLM research bias.* Journal of Marketing Management.
  - **Type:** Peer-reviewed review
  - **Importance:** Notes that much academic literature in the field is industry-tied and downplays risks, including pyramid-scheme-adjacent characteristics.

## 5. Major research, reviews and regulatory decisions

### Landmark cases

- **FTC v. Amway (1979)**
  - Ruled Amway was not a pyramid scheme because of:
    1. No inventory-loading requirement.
    2. Buy-back policy for unsold inventory.
    3. 70% rule requiring sales before reordering.
  - These safeguards became the benchmark for distinguishing legal MLM from pyramid schemes.

- **FTC v. BurnLounge (2015)**
  - Ninth Circuit held BurnLounge was a pyramid scheme despite product sales.
  - Key holding: rewards must be tied to sales to ultimate users, not merely to purchases by participants in the business opportunity.
  - Strengthened the "ultimate user" test.

- **FTC v. Herbalife (2016)**
  - $200 million consumer redress.
  - Compensation restructured to reward verifiable retail sales.
  - Required transparent, verified income disclosures.
  - Demonstrated that major, publicly traded MLMs can cross the pyramid line.

- **FTC v. TelexFree (2014/2015)**
  - Internet-based "voice over IP" pyramid scheme.
  - Court appointed receiver; billions of dollars in alleged fraud.
  - Showed how online tools can be used to operate massive pyramid schemes.

- **FTC v. Vemma (2015/2016)**
  - Nutrition-supplement MLM found to operate as a pyramid scheme.
  - Required restructuring of compensation and prohibited certain recruitment-focused practices.
  - Particularly relevant to supplement-based MLMs like NeoLife.

### Regulatory guidance / red flags

Common regulatory warning signs for pyramid schemes:
1. Emphasis on recruiting over product sales.
2. Large up-front fees or inventory-loading requirements.
3. Promises of high returns for little effort or risk.
4. Complex commission structures that obscure income sources.
5. Pressure to buy training, marketing materials, or starter kits.
6. No demonstrated demand from genuine retail customers.
7. Inventory buy-back policies are absent or restrictive.

Sources: FTC, Konsumentverket, SEC, and multiple state attorneys general.

### Peer-reviewed / independent research

- **Groß & Keep (2025)**
  - Reviewed MLM/direct-selling literature and found substantial industry ties and risk-downplaying bias; relevant because pyramid-scheme risk is often minimised in industry-affiliated research.

- **Bosley (2021)**
  - Income-disclosure review indirectly supports pyramid-scheme risk analysis: if most income derives from recruitment, then most participants cannot profit.

## 7. Established scientific / regulatory consensus

1. **Pyramid schemes are illegal in the U.S., EU, Sweden, and most jurisdictions.** They are distinguished from legal direct selling/MLM by the source of compensation.
2. **The central legal test is whether compensation derives primarily from genuine retail sales to ultimate users or from recruitment.** This is often called the "product-sales test."
3. **The "Amway safeguards" remain the reference framework for legal MLM:** no inventory loading, buy-back policy, and retail customer rule.
4. **Product-based pyramid schemes are a major modern enforcement target.** The existence of products is necessary but not sufficient for legitimacy.
5. **Common red flags include:** heavy recruitment focus, inventory loading, large up-front fees, promises of easy high returns, and complex or opaque compensation.
6. **FTC enforcement has increasingly focused on income claims and disclosure practices,** recognising that misleading earnings claims are often how pyramid schemes recruit victims.
7. **Swedish consumers can report suspected pyramid schemes to Konsumentverket and, for criminal matters, to police.** The Konsumentombudsmannen may bring civil cases under the Marketing Act.
8. **Pyramid schemes can cause significant financial harm, especially when they target vulnerable populations,** but reliable cross-jurisdictional loss estimates are limited.
9. **Academic research on pyramid-scheme identification is limited;** much literature is legal/regulatory or advocacy-based rather than empirical social science.
10. **MLM and pyramid scheme are not synonyms.** While some MLMs may be prosecuted as pyramid schemes, legitimate MLM that sells genuine products to retail customers remains legal.

## 8. Areas of controversy

1. **Where exactly is the line between aggressive MLM and pyramid scheme?** Regulators apply case-by-case facts; some MLMs operate in grey zones.
2. **How should "internal consumption" by distributors be counted?** Industry argues it is legitimate retail demand; critics argue it can mask inventory loading.
3. **Can small-scale personal-network direct selling avoid pyramid classification?** Low-pressure sales among friends may not raise pyramid concerns, but compensation structure still matters.
4. **Are national regulators adequately resourced to police pyramid schemes?** Consumer advocates often argue enforcement is insufficient.
5. **Should crypto/blockchain compensation plans be treated as pyramid schemes?** New technology has created hybrid models not fully addressed by traditional MLM case law.
6. **What is the appropriate role for DSA self-regulation in preventing pyramid schemes?** DSA codes exist but critics question enforcement.
7. **Cross-border enforcement.** Many schemes operate online across jurisdictions, complicating prosecution.
8. **Cognitive and social vulnerability.** Research on who is most susceptible and how to protect them is limited.

## 9. Common misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction / nuance |
|---|---|
| "All MLMs are pyramid schemes." | Pyramid schemes are illegal; MLM that meets the product-sales test is legal. The legal distinction matters. |
| "If products are sold, it is not a pyramid scheme." | Product existence is necessary but not sufficient. Compensation must derive from retail sales, not primarily recruitment. |
| "Pyramid schemes are obvious scams that everyone can spot." | Many schemes use professional marketing, testimonials, and product masking to appear legitimate. |
| "Pyramid schemes and Ponzi schemes are the same." | Ponzi schemes pay returns from new investors; pyramid schemes rely on recruitment tiers. Differentstructures, both illegal. |
| "Legality means a business is safe to join." | A company may be legal yet still offer poor earnings prospects for typical participants. |
| "You can identify a pyramid by its compensation plan shape." | Shape (binary, matrix, etc.) does not determine legality; whether compensation is recruitment-driven does. |
| "Only greedy or naive people join pyramid schemes." | Schemes often exploit social relationships, financial distress, and trust in friends/family. |
| "Countries ban pyramid schemes easily." | Enforcement is resource-intensive and often reactive; new schemes appear faster than old ones are shut down. |

## Factual consistency notes

- **Pyramid schemes and MLM are not the same.** Pyramid schemes are illegal by definition; MLM can be legal if it meets the product-sales test.
- **Product existence does not prevent pyramid classification.** BurnLounge and Herbalife demonstrate that product-based schemes can be prosecuted.
- **The legal test focuses on the source of compensation, not the shape of the organisation chart.** Binary, matrix, and unilevel structures are neither legal nor illegal by themselves.
- **Ponzi and pyramid schemes are distinct.** Use precise terminology.
- **Do not give legal advice.** Swedish consumers should be directed to Konsumentverket or a legal professional.
- **Avoid implying all MLMs are pyramid schemes or that all are safe.** Both extremes are factually inaccurate.
