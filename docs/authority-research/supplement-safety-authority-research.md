# Kosttillskott säkerhet — Authority Research Library

**Topic:** Är kosttillskott farligt? Risker, biverkningar och interaktioner
**Language:** Swedish
**Date:** 2026-06-30
**Research confidence:** High
**Scope:** This document summarizes the current scientific and regulatory authority landscape for dietary supplement safety as used by LevNytt's content cluster. It is a research input, not a finished article. Covers supplement risks for the general Swedish consumer — not sports doping, bodybuilding, or prescription-level nutraceuticals.

---

## 1. Topic

Dietary supplements (kosttillskott) are concentrated sources of nutrients or other substances with nutritional or physiological effect, intended to supplement the normal diet. In Sweden, they are regulated as food — not as pharmaceuticals. This creates a fundamentally different safety framework from medicines. The central consumer question — "Är kosttillskott farligt?" — requires understanding the real but often overstated risks, the specific products and populations where risk is elevated, and the regulatory safeguards that do (and do not) exist.

This entry covers: regulatory classification in Sweden/EU, overdose/toxicity risk for specific nutrients, contamination and adulteration, herb-induced liver injury (HILI), drug-supplement interactions, vulnerable populations, the scientific evidence on harm vs benefit, and persistent knowledge gaps.

**Overall assessment:** Confidence is high that most supplements on the Swedish market are safe when used according to label directions by healthy adults. Confidence is also high that specific risks exist — overdose of fat-soluble vitamins and certain minerals, hepatotoxicity from specific botanicals, drug interactions, and contamination. The key public health message is nuance: most products are safe, some are not, and risk depends on product, dose, individual health status, and concurrent medication.

## 3. Regulatory framework

### 3.1 EU regulatory basis

- **Directive 2002/46/EC** — The EU Food Supplements Directive. Harmonises lists of vitamins and minerals permitted in supplements, labelling requirements, and definitions. Does NOT establish mandatory maximum levels for vitamins and minerals.
  - Type: EU directive
  - Importance: Foundational legal framework. No binding maximum limits have been enacted despite the directive envisaging them.

- **Regulation (EC) No 178/2002** — General Food Law. Establishes the principle that food (including supplements) placed on the market must be safe. Producer bears primary responsibility.
  - Type: EU regulation
  - Importance: Establishes the "producer responsibility" principle. A 2018 Swedish Supreme Administrative Court ruling (HFD 2018 ref. 65) interpreted this to mean that in the absence of EU maximum limits, supplements cannot be deemed unsafe solely on the basis of high nutrient levels.

- **Regulation (EU) No 432/2012** — Authorised health claims list. Supplements may only use EFSA-authorised health claims.
  - Type: EU regulation
  - Importance: Prevents medicinal claims. Supplements cannot claim to treat, prevent, or cure disease.

- **Regulation (EU) 2023/915** — Maximum levels for certain contaminants in food. Sets limits for heavy metals, mycotoxins, and other contaminants.
  - Type: EU regulation
  - Importance: Establishes legal limits for contaminants applicable to supplements.

### 3.2 Swedish national regulation

- **LIVSFS 2023:3** — Livsmedelsverkets föreskrifter om kosttillskott. Replaces LIVSFS 2003:9 (effective 1 July 2023). Specifies labelling requirements, definition, and compositional rules.
  - Type: National regulation
  - Importance: Primary Swedish regulatory text. No substantive changes from 2003 version — update of references, language, and structure.

- **No pre-market approval system exists in Sweden.** Unlike pharmaceuticals, supplements do not require authorisation from Läkemedelsverket before sale. Producers must register with the local municipal control authority. Animal-derived supplements (e.g., fish oil capsules) require Livsmedelsverket approval.

- **Livsmedelsverket Kontrollwiki** — Provides inspection guidance for municipal authorities. Covers risk assessment methodology, UL-based evaluation, and enforcement practice.

### 3.3 Scientific risk assessment

- **EFSA Tolerable Upper Intake Levels (ULs)** — Science-based reference values for the maximum chronic daily intake of a nutrient from all sources judged unlikely to pose a risk of adverse health effects.
  - Updated framework published 2024 (EFSA NDA Panel, 2024). Guidance document: EFSA Journal 2024;22(11):e9052.
  - Current ULs cover: vitamin A, vitamin D, vitamin E, vitamin C, thiamin, riboflavin, niacin, vitamin B6, folate/folic acid, vitamin B12, biotin, pantothenic acid, calcium, magnesium, phosphorus, iron, zinc, copper, iodine, selenium, manganese, molybdenum, fluoride, chromium, boron, nickel, tin, vanadium.
  - For some nutrients (e.g., vitamin K, chromium, manganese), no UL can be established due to insufficient data. A "safe level of intake" is provided instead.
  - **Importance:** Primary scientific reference for safety assessment in the EU. Used by Livsmedelsverket as the benchmark for evaluating supplement safety.

- **Nordic Nutrition Recommendations 2023 (NNR2023)** — Section on kosttillskott states: "Bland friska personer som äter en varierad och energimässigt tillräcklig kost har långvarigt intag av näringsämnen från kosttillskott generellt inte visats vara hälsofrämjande. Tvärtom finns det evidens för att högt intag av vissa kosttillskott, framför allt vitaminer med antioxidativa egenskaper, skulle kunna leda till negativa hälsoeffekter såsom ökad risk för förtida död."
  - Type: Regional evidence-based guideline
  - Importance: Highest Nordic authority on nutrient requirements. Directly supports nuanced supplement messaging.

### 3.4 Swedish Supreme Administrative Court ruling (HFD 2018 ref. 65)

- **Ruling:** In the absence of EU-harmonised maximum levels for vitamins and minerals in supplements, a product cannot be deemed unsafe under Article 14(7) of Regulation 178/2002 solely on the basis of high nutrient content.
- **Importance:** This ruling effectively prevents Swedish authorities from banning or restricting supplements based on dose alone. Livsmedelsverket continues to recommend that producers use EFSA ULs as a voluntary benchmark. The ruling has been criticised by public health advocates for creating a regulatory gap.

## 5. Major systematic reviews and clinical studies

### 5.1 Adverse events — general population

- **Geller, A.I. et al. (2015).** *Emergency Department Visits for Adverse Events Related to Dietary Supplements.* New England Journal of Medicine 373:1531–1540.
  - Finding: Estimated 23,000 ED visits/year in the US for supplement-related adverse events. Weight-loss and energy supplements disproportionately implicated. Young adults (20–34) most affected for weight-loss/energy supplements; older adults for micronutrient supplements (especially choking/ swallowing issues).
  - Importance: Population-level quantification. Key reference for the message that adverse events from supplements are a real public health issue, though rare relative to total consumption.

- **Ronis, M.J.J., Pedersen, K.B. & Watt, J. (2018).** *Adverse Effects of Nutraceuticals and Dietary Supplements.* Annual Review of Pharmacology and Toxicology 58:583–601.
  - Finding: Supplements are not necessarily safe for everybody. Active ingredients with physiological effects can cause adverse effects in susceptible individuals. Risk of drug-supplement interactions is significant due to under-disclosure by patients.
  - Importance: Authoritative review covering mechanisms, toxic effects, and drug interactions across supplement classes.

- **Rathi, H. et al. (2026).** *Nutrivigilance: a critical evaluation of post-market safety surveillance in nutritional and dietary products.* LMRR.
  - Finding: Accumulating evidence indicates clinically relevant adverse events including hepatotoxicity, nephrotoxicity, cardiovascular disturbances, endocrine dysfunction, and serious interactions with prescription medications.
  - Importance: Recent review advocating for systematic post-market surveillance akin to pharmacovigilance.

### 5.2 Hepatotoxicity (HILI)

- **Navarro, V.J. et al. (2014).** *Liver injury from herbals and dietary supplements in the U.S. Drug-Induced Liver Injury Network.* Hepatology 60(4):1399–1408.
  - Finding: HDS accounted for 7% of DILI cases in 2004–2005, rising to 20% by 2013–2014. Herbalife, Hydroxycut, and green tea extract most commonly implicated.
  - Importance: Landmark paper documenting the rising proportion of HDS-related liver injury.

- **Fontana, R.J. et al. (2022).** *AASLD practice guidance on drug, herbal, and dietary supplement–induced liver injury.* Hepatology 77(3):1036–1065.
  - Finding: Comprehensive clinical guidance. HILI diagnosis remains challenging — no validated biomarkers, diagnosis of exclusion. Recommends systematic causality assessment.
  - Importance: Current clinical gold standard for diagnosing supplement-related liver injury.

- **Ballotin, V.R. et al. (2021).** *Herb-induced liver injury: Systematic review and meta-analysis.* World Journal of Clinical Cases 9:5490–5513.
  - Finding: 79 types of herbs/herbal compounds linked to HILI. Most common: He-Shou-Wu, green tea extract, Herbalife, kava kava, greater celandine, germander, Hydroxycut, ashwagandha. 82.8% recovered; 6.6% required liver transplant; 10.4% death.
  - Importance: Comprehensive systematic review. Quantifies outcomes at the severe end of the spectrum.

- **Rivero-Pino, F. & Casanova, A.G. (2023).** *Hepatotoxicity due to dietary supplements: state-of-the-art, gaps and perspectives.* Critical Reviews in Toxicology.
  - Finding: Supplements described as hepatotoxic are mainly of botanical origin (green tea, turmeric) and sports supplements (anabolic androgenic steroids). Prevalence difficult to define due to underreporting.
  - Importance: Recent review highlighting gaps in evidence and the challenge of establishing causality.

- **Lin, J.K.-S. & Tujios, S.R. (2023).** *Hidden Dangers: Herbal and Dietary Supplement Induced Hepatotoxicity.* Livers 4(1).
  - Finding: HDS-induced acute liver failure resulted in transplant or death more often than prescription medication cases (83% vs 66%). Over 18% of acute liver failure cases attributed to HDS, with 50% resulting in death or transplant.
  - Importance: Highlights the severe end of the harm spectrum. Important for balanced risk communication.

- **Carty, J. & Navarro, V.J. (2024).** *Dietary Supplement-Induced Hepatotoxicity: A Clinical Perspective.* Journal of Dietary Supplements.
  - Finding: Notable examples include garcinia cambogia, turmeric, and ashwagandha. Causality assessment tools are crucial but have limitations for multi-ingredient products.
  - Importance: Current clinical perspective including emerging supplements of concern (ashwagandha).

### 5.3 Cancer risk from supplements

- **MDPI Cancers (2024).** *Supplement Use and Increased Risks of Cancer: Unveiling the Other Side of the Coin.* Cancers 16(5):880.
  - Finding: Beyond known toxicities at high levels, dietary supplements may contribute to increased risk of cancer. Evidence from SELECT trial (vitamin E/selenium and prostate cancer), ATBC (beta-carotene and lung cancer in smokers).
  - Importance: The "more is better" mentality is not supported. Some antioxidant supplements have shown unexpected harm in large trials.

- **SELECT trial (2008–2011).** Selenium and Vitamin E Cancer Prevention Trial. JAMA.
  - Finding: Vitamin E (400 IU/day) increased prostate cancer risk by 17% in healthy men. No benefit from selenium.
  - Importance: One of the largest and most cited examples of supplement harm from a well-conducted RCT.

- **ATBC Study (Alpha-Tocopherol, Beta-Carotene Cancer Prevention Study).** NEJM 1994.
  - Finding: Beta-carotene supplementation (20 mg/day) increased lung cancer risk by 18% in Finnish male smokers.
  - Importance: Landmark finding. Beta-carotene supplements are now known to be harmful for smokers and asbestos-exposed individuals.

### 5.4 Drug-supplement interactions

- **Livsmedelsverket risk page:** Lists known interactions including:
  - Vitamin K and warfarin (anticoagulants)
  - St. John's Wort and multiple drugs (CYP450 induction)
  - Calcium and iron (mutual absorption interference)
  - Magnesium and certain antibiotics
  - Grapefruit/grapefruit extract and statins, calcium channel blockers
- **EVM (UK Expert Group on Vitamins and Minerals, 2003).** *Safe Upper Levels for Vitamins and Minerals.*
  - Importance: Although older, still a key reference for interaction risk assessment.

### 5.5 Contamination and adulteration

- **Livsmedelsverket:** Reports of ayurveda products containing heavy metals (arsenic, lead, mercury), undeclared pharmaceuticals (sibutramine in weight-loss products, sildenafil in sexual-enhancement products), anabolic steroids in sports supplements, and mycotoxins (aflatoxins, ochratoxin) in botanical ingredients.
- **Cohen, P.A. (2014 et seq.).** Multiple publications documenting undeclared pharmaceutical ingredients in supplements sold in the US.
  - Finding: Contamination and adulteration are persistent problems, particularly in products from outside the EU.

| Claim | Hierarchy | Representative evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Most supplements on the Swedish market are safe at label doses for healthy adults | **Regulatory consensus** | Livsmedelsverket, EFSA |
| Overdose risk exists for fat-soluble vitamins (A, D) and certain minerals (selenium, iodine, iron) | **Established mechanism and guideline** | EFSA ULs, NNR2023 |

| Certain botanical supplements can cause liver injury (HILI) | **Moderate-high evidence** | Systematic reviews: Ballotin 2021, DILIN, AASLD guidance |
| High-dose beta-carotene supplements increase lung cancer risk in smokers | **Strong RCT evidence** | ATBC study (NEJM 1994) |
| High-dose vitamin E (400 IU/day) may increase prostate cancer risk | **Moderate RCT evidence** | SELECT trial (JAMA 2011) |
| Supplement-Drug interactions are clinically relevant | **Established; underreported** | Livsmedelsverket, EVM, case series |

## 7. Established scientific consensus

1. **Most supplements are safe when used as directed** by healthy adults. Livsmedelsverket: "De flesta kosttillskott som säljs i Sverige kan du som vuxen och frisk äta utan risk."
2. **Some supplements can cause harm.** Risk increases with dose, duration, poly-supplement use, and individual vulnerability (medications, liver/kidney disease, pregnancy).
3. **Overdose risk exists** for fat-soluble vitamins (A, D) and trace minerals (selenium, iodine, iron) — the margin between adequate intake and harmful dose is narrow for these nutrients.
4. **Certain botanicals carry established hepatotoxicity risk** — green tea extract (high-dose), kava kava, ashwagandha, turmeric (high-dose), greater celandine, germander. The risk is idiosyncratic (unpredictable, not dose-dependent) for most.
5. **Contamination and adulteration are persistent problems** — particularly for products from outside the EU regulatory system, weight-loss and sports supplements, and ayurveda products.
6. **Drug-supplement interactions are under-recognized** by both consumers and healthcare providers. Key interactions involve anticoagulants (vitamin K, St. John's Wort, ginkgo, high-dose fish oil), thyroid medications (calcium, iron, soy), and multiple CYP450-metabolised drugs (St. John's Wort).
7. **"Natural" does not equal "safe."** Many of the most hepatotoxic supplements are botanical products perceived as harmless.
8. **The "more is better" assumption is false.** Large RCTs show that high-dose antioxidant supplements can cause harm (ATBC, SELECT, meta-analyses).
9. **Underreporting of adverse events is severe.** Estimates suggest <1% of supplement-related adverse events are reported to authorities. True population harm incidence is unknown.

## 8. Areas where evidence is mixed or uncertain

1. **True incidence of supplement-related harm at population level.** Underreporting makes estimation impossible. ED visit data (Geller 2015) provides a floor estimate only.
2. **Causality in HILI cases.** Multi-ingredient products make specific attribution difficult. No validated biomarkers exist. RUCAM has limitations for HDS.
3. **Long-term cancer risk from specific nutrients** at supplement doses. Associations exist (beta-carotene, vitamin E, selenium) but not all nutrients have been studied and mechanisms are incompletely understood.
4. **Threshold for selenium toxicity.** Individual susceptibility varies. The margin between RDI (~55 μg/day) and UL (300 μg/day) is wide, but some populations may be at risk at lower levels.
5. **Interaction between supplements and specific medications.** Most interactions are documented through case reports, not systematic studies. Many potential interactions remain uncharacterised.
6. **Prevalence of contamination in the EU supplement market.** Systematic testing data is not publicly available at scale. Point-prevalence studies suggest contamination is not rare but its extent is unknown.
7. **Effect of long-term, low-dose exposure to multiple supplements simultaneously.** Most safety studies examine single nutrients. Real-world use often involves multi-product regimens.

## 9. Common misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction / nuance |
|---|---|
| "Kosttillskott är alltid ofarliga eftersom de säljs i mataffären." | Supplements are regulated as food, not medicine. There is no pre-market safety approval. The producer bears sole responsibility for safety. |
| "Naturligt är alltid säkert." | Many of the most toxic substances are natural. Botanical supplements like kava, green tea extract, and ashwagandha have documented hepatotoxicity. |
| "Mer är bättre — extra skydd." | High-dose supplements, especially antioxidants, have shown harm in large RCTs. More is not better and can be worse. |
| "Man behöver inte berätta för läkaren om kosttillskott." | Supplement-drug interactions are real and underreported. Always inform your doctor, especially if taking prescription medications. |
| "Alla kosttillskott är testade och godkända." | There is no mandatory testing or approval. Some products contain undeclared pharmaceuticals, heavy metals, or incorrect ingredient levels. |
| "Vitaminer kan inte vara farliga — kroppen kissar ut överskottet." | True for water-soluble vitamins (C, B-complex) but NOT for fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) or minerals. These accumulate in the body. |
| "Man behöver inte bry sig om doseringen." | The recommended daily dose on the label is the producer's safety assessment. Exceeding it increases risk without proven additional benefit. |
| "Kosttillskott kan kompensera för en dålig kost." | NNR 2023: "Det finns ingen vetenskapligt underbyggd anledning att låta kosttillskott kompensera en obalanserad kost." |
| "Alla växtbaserade kosttillskott är harmlösa." | The most hepatotoxic supplements are often botanical. Green tea extract, kava, ashwagandha, and turmeric have all been linked to liver injury. |

## Factual consistency notes

- The statement "de flesta kosttillskott som säljs i Sverige kan du som vuxen och frisk äta utan risk" is Livsmedelsverket's official position. It must be presented with the caveat that individual products vary widely.
- The HFD 2018 ruling means there are no legally binding maximum doses for vitamins and minerals in Swedish supplements. This is a regulatory fact, not an endorsement of high doses.
- HILI statistics (6.6% transplant rate, 10.4% death) come from Ballotin 2021 systematic review and reflect the severe end of reported cases only. They do not represent population-level risk for all supplement users.
- "23,000 ED visits/year" from Geller 2015 is US-specific. No equivalent Swedish data exists.
- The SELECT trial's 17% prostate cancer increase is for 400 IU/day vitamin E in men over 50. This dose is substantially higher than most multivitamin products (typically 30 IU).
- NNR 2023: "ökad risk för förtida död" refers to high-dose antioxidant supplements specifically, not all supplements. Should not be generalized.

## Source labelling convention for downstream articles

| Tag | Meaning |

| **Guideline** | Official recommendation (Livsmedelsverket, EFSA, NNR, WHO). |
