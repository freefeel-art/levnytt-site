## 1. Topic

The **U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC)** is the primary federal civil regulator of multi-level marketing in the United States. Through enforcement actions, rulemaking, and consumer guidance, the FTC has shaped the legal framework that distinguishes legitimate MLM from illegal pyramid schemes and protects consumers from deceptive business-opportunity claims.

### Core definitions

| Term | Definition | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **FTC Act (15 U.S.C. § 45)** | Statute prohibiting unfair or deceptive acts or practices in commerce. | Primary federal statutory basis for FTC MLM enforcement. |
| **Section 5** | The core prohibition against unfair or deceptive practices. | Used against misleading income claims and pyramid schemes. |
| **Pyramid scheme** | Business model where compensation derives primarily from recruitment, not retail sales. | Illegal under FTC Act Section 5. |
| **Business Opportunity Rule** | FTC rule requiring disclosures for certain business-opportunity sales. | Applies to many but not all MLMs. |
| **Consent order / settlement** | Administrative agreement resolving enforcement action without admission of guilt. | Common FTC resolution (e.g., Herbalife, Vemma). |
| **Administrative complaint / Part 3 proceeding** | Formal FTC litigation before an administrative law judge. | FTC v. Amway followed this path. |
| **Redress / restitution** | Return of money to harmed consumers. | Used in settlements (e.g., $200 million in Herbalife). |

## 3. Tier 0 original sources

### Statutes

- **15 U.S.C. § 45 (FTC Act, Section 5).**
  - **Type:** Federal statute
  - **Importance:** Prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices; the foundational authority for all FTC MLM enforcement.

- **15 U.S.C. § 68 (Pyramid Promotional Schemes — context).**
  - **Type:** Federal statute
  - **Importance:** While not a standalone pyramid criminal statute at federal level, related provisions support FTC and DOJ enforcement.

### FTC rules

- **FTC Business Opportunity Rule (16 C.F.R. Part 437), 2012.**
  - **Type:** Federal trade regulation rule
  - **Importance:** Requires a one-page disclosure for covered business opportunities, including earnings claims, litigation history, and cancellation/refund policies.

- **FTC Guides Concerning the Use of Endorsements and Testimonials in Advertising (2009, updated).**
  - **Type:** interpretive guidance
  - **Importance:** Governs use of income testimonials and atypical-result disclosures.

### Landmark cases

- **FTC v. Amway Corp. (1979).**
  - **Type:** Administrative decision
  - **Importance:** Established the "Amway safeguards" and product-sales test.

- **FTC v. BurnLounge, Inc. (9th Cir. 2015).**
  - **Type:** Federal appellate decision
  - **Importance:** Clarified the ultimate-user test for product-based pyramid schemes.

- **FTC v. Herbalife (2016 consent order).**
  - **Type:** Administrative settlement
  - **Importance:** $200 million consumer redress; compensation restructuring; independent compliance monitor.

- **FTC v. Vemma (2015 preliminary injunction; 2016 settlement).**
  - **Type:** Enforcement action and settlement
  - **Importance:** Nutrition MLM found to operate as pyramid scheme; recruitment-focused compensation prohibited.

- **FTC v. TelexFree (2014/2015).**
  - **Type:** Enforcement action
  - **Importance:** Internet-based pyramid scheme; receiver appointed; massive consumer losses.

### Consumer guidance

- **FTC Consumer Alert (September 4, 2024).** *What are multi-level marketing (MLM) disclosure statements really telling you?*
  - **Type:** Government publication
  - **Importance:** Current FTC staff analysis of IDS and income claims.

- **FTC Consumer Information — Multi-Level Marketing Businesses and Schemes.**
  - **Type:** Government guidance
  - **Importance:** Practical consumer advice on evaluating MLM opportunities.

## 5. Major research, reviews and regulatory decisions

### FTC v. Amway (1979)

- **Holding:** Amway's multi-level marketing plan was not an illegal pyramid scheme.
- **Reasoning:**
  1. No requirement to purchase inventory to participate.
  2. 90% buy-back policy for unsold inventory.
  3. 70% rule: distributors had to sell at least 70% of previously purchased products before reordering.
- **Legacy:** These "Amway safeguards" remain the reference framework for MLM legitimacy.

### FTC v. BurnLounge (2015)

- **Holding:** 9th Circuit affirmed that BurnLounge operated as a pyramid scheme.
- **Key test:** Compensation must be tied to sales to ultimate users, not merely to purchases by participants in the opportunity.
- **Significance:** Product-based pyramid schemes can be illegal even if some products are sold.

### FTC v. Herbalife (2016)

- **Settlement terms:**
  - $200 million in consumer redress.
  - Compensation must reward verified retail sales.
  - Clear, honest income disclosures required.
  - Independent compliance auditor for seven years.
- **Significance:** Largest MLM settlement to date; demonstrated FTC willingness to pursue major companies.

### FTC v. Vemma (2015/2016)

- **Findings:** Vemma's compensation plan was focused on recruitment and inventory purchases by affiliates.
- **Outcome:** Preliminary injunction; settlement requiring restructuring.
- **Relevance to supplement MLMs:** Vemma sold nutrition products, making it similar in product category to NeoLife.

### FTC v. TelexFree

- **Scheme:** Promoted as an internet-phone service but functioned as a pyramid.
- **Outcome:** Receiver appointed; SEC and criminal actions followed; multi-billion-dollar alleged fraud.
- **Significance:** Cross-border internet-based scheme; highlighted role of financial regulators.

### FTC Business Opportunity Rule

- Requires a disclosure document for covered sellers.
- Includes:
  - Identifying information
  - Earnings claims (if made)
  - Litigation/criminal history
  - Refund/cancellation policy
- **Limitation:** Does not cover all MLMs because some compensation models do not fit the rule's definition of a "business opportunity."

## 7. Established scientific / regulatory consensus

1. **The FTC has broad authority to prohibit unfair or deceptive MLM practices under Section 5 of the FTC Act.**
2. **The core legal test for pyramid schemes is whether compensation derives primarily from recruitment or from retail sales to ultimate users.**
3. **The Amway safeguards (no inventory loading, buy-back policy, retail-customer rule) remain the benchmark for legal MLM.**
4. **Product-based pyramid schemes can be prosecuted; the existence of products alone is not enough to legalise a recruitment-driven model.**
5. **Income claims by MLMs must be truthful, substantiated, and not misleading.** This applies across FTC Act, Business Opportunity Rule, and endorsement guides.
6. **FTC enforcement is typically reactive:** investigations begin after complaints or market monitoring reveal problems.
7. **Settlements such as Herbalife include structural remedies (compensation restructuring, monitorship, redress), not just fines.**
8. **The FTC works with state attorneys general, the SEC, and international agencies on large or cross-border schemes.**
9. **The FTC does not pre-approve MLM companies or compensation plans.** Consumers should not interpret silence as endorsement.
10. **FTC consumer guidance is a practical resource for evaluating MLM opportunities and recognising pyramid-scheme red flags.**

## 8. Areas of controversy

1. **Is FTC enforcement adequate?** Consumer advocates argue the FTC is under-resourced; industry argues existing enforcement is sufficient.
2. **Should the Business Opportunity Rule be broadened to cover all MLMs?** The FTC excluded some MLMs from the 2012 rule; advocates call for broader coverage.
3. **What is the appropriate role of state vs federal enforcement?** Some high-profile cases are state-led.
4. **Should there be a federal criminal pyramid statute?** Currently most criminal cases use fraud, securities, or money-laundering statutes.
5. **Does FTC action damage legitimate MLMs?** Industry groups argueoverbroad enforcement har chilling effects; critics say enforcement is too narrow.
6. **How should international MLMs be regulated?** Many companies operate across borders; FTC jurisdiction is limited to U.S. conduct.
7. **Are consent orders effective deterrents?** Some settlements are seen as costly but not transformative; monitoring is resource-intensive.
8. **What is the role of Republican vs Democratic FTC leadership?** Enforcement intensity and rulemaking priorities can shift with political control.

## 9. Common misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction / nuance |
|---|---|
| "The FTC approves MLM companies." | The FTC does not pre-approve; it investigates and prosecutes when it finds violations. |
| "If the FTC has not shut down a company, it must be legal." | Lack of enforcement action does not mean a company is legal or safe. |
| "FTC v. Amway made all MLMs legal." | Amway established a legal framework; individual companies must still meet the product-sales test. |
| "Pyramid schemes are only prosecuted criminally." | The FTC primarily uses civil enforcement; criminal cases are handled by DOJ. |
| "The FTC only targets small or foreign MLMs." | Major companies such as Herbalife have been subject to significant FTC action. |
| "A settlement means the company admitted guilt." | Consent orders typically do not include admissions of guilt. |
| "FTC rules guarantee consumers get their money back." | Redress depends on available funds and is often partial. |
| "FTC enforcement applies globally." | The FTC's direct authority is mainly within the United States, though it may coordinate internationally. |

## Factual consistency notes

- **The FTC does not pre-approve MLM companies or plans.** Avoid language implying FTC certification.
- **Consent orders do not typically include admissions of guilt.** Settlements are not judicial findings of liability.
- **Business Opportunity Rule does not cover all MLMs.** Be precise about scope.
- **BurnLounge and Amway are complementary precedents.** Amway shows what can be legal; BurnLounge shows what can be illegal despite products.
- **FTC jurisdiction is primarily U.S.-based.** For Swedish readers, link to Konsumentverket and EU law for local remedies.
- **Distinguish civil FTC enforcement from criminal prosecution.** DOJ handles criminal pyramid cases.
