## 1. Topic

Network marketing — also called **multi-level marketing (MLM)** or, in Swedish, **nätverksmarknadsföring** or **nätverksförsäljning** — is a distribution model in which independent sellers (distributors) earn income from their own product sales and, in many companies, from the sales of people they recruit into their organisation.

### Core definitions

| Term | Definition | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct selling** | Sale of goods/services away from fixed retail locations, typically person-to-person or through personal contacts. | Broad umbrella that includes single-level door-to-door sales, party-plan selling, and MLM. |
| **Network marketing** | A form of direct selling in which distributors build a network of other distributors and may earn override commissions on their organisation's sales. | Often used as a less stigmatised synonym for MLM. |
| **Multi-level marketing (MLM)** | Direct selling with a compensation plan that pays distributors on multiple levels of recruitment/sales. | Legally defined by whether compensation is principally tied to product sales or to recruitment. |
| **Pyramid scheme** | An illegal scheme in which participants earn primarily from recruiting new participants, often via fees or inventory purchases, rather than from genuine retail sales. | Illegal in the EU, US, and most jurisdictions; the legal boundary with MLM is central to regulation. |
| **Direct selling association (DSA)** | National or regional trade organisations that represent direct selling companies, publish ethics codes, and collect industry statistics. | Includes WFDSA globally and national DSAs (e.g., in the US, UK, Sweden). |

### Focus of this library entry

This entry covers:
- The distinction between direct selling, network marketing, and MLM
- Historical development and landmark cases
- Major regulators, trade bodies, and research authorities
- Tier 0 original sources
- Regulatory frameworks (US, EU, Sweden)

Network marketing is not a biomedical topic, so "scientific consensus" here refers to the legal/regulatory consensus, peer-reviewed social-science and economic findings, and the documented limits of industry economic claims.

## 3. Tier 0 original sources

> Tier 0 = original research, official regulatory documents, court decisions, legislation, and primary publications from influential figures. Prefer these over summaries.

### Regulatory / legal / government

- **FTC Consumer Alert (September 4, 2024).** *What are multi-level marketing (MLM) disclosure statements really telling you?*
  - **Type:** Original government publication
  - **Importance:** Most recent FTC staff analysis of MLM income disclosures; identifies common misleading tactics and gives concrete income distribution data.
  - **Key use:** Support claims that most MLM participants earn little or nothing and that disclosures can be misleading.

- **FTC v. Amway Corp. (1979).**
  - **Type:** Administrative law decision
  - **Importance:** Established the legal boundary between MLM and pyramid schemes via the "Amway safeguards" (no required inventory loading, buy-back policy, 70% retail-sales rule).

- **FTC / Herbalife settlement (2016).**
  - **Type:** Administrative settlement with consent order
  - **Importance:** Demonstrates enforcement at scale ($200 million consumer redress, compensation restructuring, clearer income disclosures); often cited as a landmark in modern MLM regulation.

- **Marknadsföringslagen (2008:486).** *Swedish Marketing Act.*
  - **Type:** National legislation
  - **Importance:** Governs misleading marketing in Sweden, including income claims and consumer-rights issues relevant to MLM.

- **Konsumentverket guidance on pyramid schemes and direct selling.**
  - **Type:** National consumer-protection guidance
  - **Importance:** Practical Swedish-language guidance distinguishing legal MLM from illegal pyramid schemes and outlining consumer rights.

### Industry / trade association

- **World Federation of Direct Selling Associations (WFDSA). 2024 Global STATS Report.**
  - **Type:** Original industry statistics
  - **Importance:** Primary source for global direct selling sales ($163.9 billion) and distributor numbers (104.3 million, 72.1% women). Must be treated as industry self-reported data.

- **WFDSA Consumer Protection page / Code of Ethics.**
  - **Type:** Industry self-regulation document
  - **Importance:** Documents claimed industry standards (e.g., buy-back guarantees). Useful for understanding industry framing; not independent evidence of enforcement.

### Peer-reviewed research

- **Bosley, Stacey (2021).** *Multi-level marketing income disclosure statements: A review of average earnings data.*
  - **Type:** Peer-reviewed original research / review
  - **Importance:** Estimated approximately 25% of U.S. MLM sellers make a profit; provides independent academic anchor for income distribution discussions.
  - **Note:** Verify exact journal citation before final article use.

- **Groß, C. & Keep, W. (2025).** *Direct selling and multi-level marketing: A review of research bias.* Journal of Marketing Management.
  - **Type:** Peer-reviewed review
  - **Importance:** Reviewed 68 scientific articles and found 59% had direct industry ties and systematically downplayed risks; central for caveating positive MLM research.

### Historical / philosophical primary sources

- **Berkshire Hathaway Inc. Shareholder Letters archive (Warren Buffett).**
  - **Type:** Original investor communications
  - **Importance:** Sometimes cited in MLM circles because Buffett owns The Pampered Chef (a direct selling subsidiary). Tier 0 review confirms the letters do not address MLM as an industry or endorse it as a typical opportunity.

- **Eric Worre. *Go Pro: 7 Steps to Becoming a Network Marketing Professional.***
  - **Type:** Original network-marketing education book
  - **Importance:** Influential industry voice; confirms the philosophical/motivational framing of network marketing as a professional entrepreneurial vehicle. Not empirical evidence.

- **Richard Bliss Brooke. *The Four Year Career*, *Mach2*, *Leadership on Fire.***
  - **Type:** Original network-marketing education books
  - **Importance:** Influential trainer voice for LevNytt-adjacent business-opportunity content; not evidence of typical outcomes.

- **Jim Rohn official website — original seminar recordings and articles.**
  - **Type:** Original motivational/educational content
  - **Importance:** Rohn worked with Nutrilite/Amway early; his work is personal-development philosophy, not MLM outcome data.

- **Stephen Covey. *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People* / FranklinCovey course materials.**
  - **Type:** Original personal-effectiveness book/course
  - **Importance:** Personal-effectiveness framework sometimes borrowed by MLM trainers; not MLM-specific evidence.

- **John C. Maxwell. Maxwell Leadership books, courses, podcast.**
  - **Type:** Original leadership content
  - **Importance:** General leadership philosophy; not evidence of network-marketing outcomes.

## 5. Major research, reviews and regulatory decisions

### Landmark regulatory decisions

- **FTC v. Amway Corp. (1979)**
  - Established that Amway's multi-level compensation plan was not an illegal pyramid scheme because of three safeguards:
    1. No requirement to purchase inventory to participate (no inventory loading).
    2. Buy-back policy for unsold inventory.
    3. 70% rule — distributors had to sell at least 70% of previously purchased products before reordering.
  - These safeguards remain the reference framework for MLM regulation.

- **FTC v. Herbalife (2016 settlement)**
  - Herbalife agreed to pay $200 million in consumer redress.
  - Compensation was restructured to reward actual retail sales.
  - Required clear, honest disclosure of typical distributor income.
  - Demonstrates that even large, publicly traded MLMs can cross regulatory lines on income claims.

- **FTC Consumer Alert (2024)**
  - Analysis of income disclosures from dozens of MLMs.
  - Found most participants made $1,000 or less per year.
  - In at least 17 MLMs, most participants made nothing.
  - Identified disclosure tactics that obscure low earnings and high expenses.

### Peer-reviewed research

- **Bosley, Stacey (2021)**
  - Reviewed U.S. MLM income disclosures.
  - Estimated that approximately 25% of MLM sellers make a profit; the remaining 75% lose money or break even.
  - Definitions of "active distributor," "profit," and "seller" vary, affecting comparability.

- **Groß & Keep (2025), Journal of Marketing Management**
  - Reviewed 68 scientific articles on direct selling and network marketing.
  - Found 59% had direct industry ties and systematically downplayed risks.
  - Important caveats for any literature review on MLM: industry funding and access bias are significant.

### Industry research

- **WFDSA 2024 Global STATS Report**
  - Global direct selling retail sales approximately $163.9 billion (2024).
  - 104.3 million independent contractors globally.
  - 72.1% female participation.
  - Top product categories: wellness, cosmetics & personal care, household goods & durables.
  - **Limitation:** Industry self-reported; sales figures and distributor counts may include low-activity participants.

## 7. Established scientific / regulatory consensus

1. **Legitimate MLM is legal in Sweden, the EU, and the US; pyramid schemes are illegal.** The legal boundary is whether compensation is tied primarily to genuine product sales or to recruitment fees and inventory loading.
2. **Most MLM participants earn little or no profit.** This is supported by FTC income-disclosure analysis, independent research, and company-published income statements.
3. **The "Amway safeguards" remain the reference framework for legal MLM.** No inventory loading, a buy-back policy, and a retail-customer rule are key indicators used by regulators.
4. **Income claims must be honest and must not mislead consumers about typical results.** This is enforced under Swedish, EU, and U.S. marketing law.
5. **Pyramid schemes can use products as a disguise.** Product existence is necessary but not sufficient for legitimacy; compensation structure and retail-sales emphasis matter.
6. **The global direct selling industry is large and predominantly female,** but industry revenue does not equal distributor profitability. WFDSA figures describe sales and participation scale, not earnings.
7. **Academic research on MLM is limited and may be skewed by industry ties.** Groß & Keep (2025) documented this bias; positive findings should be scrutinised for conflicts of interest.
8. **Network marketing education and leadership content is motivational/philosophical, not empirical evidence of business outcomes.** Jim Rohn, Eric Worre, Richard Bliss Brooke, Stephen Covey, and John C. Maxwell provide frameworks for personal effectiveness, not proof of typical MLM success.
9. **Consumer protection depends on transparent income disclosures, cooling-off/return rights, and accessible complaint channels.** In Sweden these are governed by Marknadsföringslagen, the Distance and Doorstep Sales Act (distans- och hemförsäljningslagen), ARN, Konsumentverket, and the market courts.
10. **Supplement-based MLM products should be evaluated by the same standards as any supplement:** evidence for ingredients, quality control, price-per-dose, and regulatory compliance — not by the business model alone.

## 8. Areas of controversy

1. **Where exactly is the legal line between MLM and pyramid scheme?** Regulators apply case-by-case analysis. The product-sales test is clear in principle but can be difficult to enforce in practice when companies blur retail sales with internal consumption by distributors.
2. **What proportion of MLM participants are truly retail customers vs. failed business owners?** Classification affects both legal analysis and income statistics. Some distributors join primarily for product discounts; others join expecting significant income. These groups are often merged in disclosure data.
3. **How should internal consumption by distributors be treated?** Some argue distributor self-use is legitimate product demand; critics argue it is disguised inventory loading that funds the pyramid. This is central to many regulatory disputes.
4. **Is the MLM business model inherently exploitative, or only certain bad actors?** Anti-MLM advocates argue the model structurally harms most participants; industry advocates argue it provides flexible entrepreneurship. Evidence varies by company and jurisdiction.
5. **Do MLM supplements offer value for money?** Independent price/quality comparisons are scarce. MLM products are often premium-priced; whether the quality justifies the price is product-specific and hotly debated.
6. **How reliable is industry-funded research?** Groß & Keep (2025) found substantial industry ties; this creates ongoing controversy about which studies can be trusted.
7. **What is the impact of MLM on social relationships?** Recruitment often targets friends and family. Social-pressure and relationship-harm concerns are widely discussed but understudied in peer-reviewed literature.
8. **Should MLM income claims require stricter regulation?** The FTC 2024 report argues current disclosures are misleading; consumer advocates often call for stronger enforcement; industry groups argue self-regulation is sufficient.
9. **What are realistic alternatives to MLM for side income?** Comparisons to e-commerce, gig work, freelancing, and affiliate marketing are common but methodologically difficult because business-model risk profiles differ.
10. **MLM in Sweden specifically.** Swedish enforcement cases, court rulings, and consumer complaints are less documented in English-language sources than U.S. cases. More primary Swedish-language sources would strengthen local content.

## 9. Common misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction / nuance |
|---|---|
| "MLM is just another word for pyramid scheme." | Pyramid schemes are illegal; legitimate MLM passes the product-sales test. The legal boundary is real, even if some MLMs operate close to it. |
| "Anyone can get rich in MLM if they work hard enough." | Aggregate data (FTC 2024, Bosley 2021) shows most participants earn little or nothing. Hard work does not guarantee success in a structurally skewed model. |
| "Nobody makes money in MLM." | A minority does profit. The issue is not zero earnings but the highly skewed distribution and misleading presentation of typical results. |
| "A product makes it automatically legitimate." | Product existence is necessary but not sufficient. Compensation must be tied primarily to retail sales, not recruitment or inventory loading. |
| "Warren Buffett endorses MLM." | Buffett owns The Pampered Chef (a direct selling subsidiary), but his shareholder letters do not discuss MLM as an industry or endorse it as a typical opportunity. |
| "MLM products are always overpriced scams." | Price and quality vary by company. Some MLM products may be comparable to premium retail products; others are not. Independent comparison data is limited. |
| "MLM supplements are scientifically proven to be better." | Health claims for MLM supplements are subject to the same evidence standard as any supplement (EFSA, FDA, FTC). The business model does not create superior science. |
| "Regulators approve MLM companies, so they must be safe." | Legality is not an investment recommendation or quality endorsement. Regulators police specific practices; they do not certify business opportunities as safe. |
| "Income disclosure statements show what I will earn." | Disclosures often present averages, omit expenses, and highlight top earners. FTC 2024 explains common misleading tactics. |
| "You can build passive income with no ongoing effort." | MLM income typically requires active selling, recruiting, training, and customer service. "Passive income" claims are generally misleading. |
| "If a company has been around for decades, it must be ethical." | Longevity can indicate regulatory compliance and market acceptance, but it does not guarantee current practices are fair or that typical participants profit. |
| "Saying no to MLM ruins friendships." | Clear, respectful boundaries are possible. Practical scripts and consumer-rights knowledge can help manage social pressure without conflict. |

## Factual consistency notes

- The **legal distinction between MLM and pyramid schemes** is an established legal principle, but individual cases are decided case-by-case. Should not be framed as "MLM is always safe" or "all MLM is fraudulent."
- The **FTC 2024 finding** that "most participants made $1,000 or less per year" / "in at least 17 MLMs most participants made nothing" applies to the MLMs reviewed; it should not be extrapolated to every MLM without qualification.
- **Bosley (2021)** estimate of ~25% of U.S. MLM sellers making a profit is a single study; definitions of "profit," "active," and "seller" vary and affect interpretation.
- **WFDSA 2024 figures** are industry self-reported and describe sales/participation scale, not distributor profitability.
- **REDUCE-IT / VITAL are omega-3 trials** and should never appear in network-marketing content. Be careful not to confuse research libraries.
- **Warren Buffett's ownership of The Pampered Chef** should not be presented as an endorsement of MLM as an industry or as proof of typical outcomes.
- **Eric Worre, Richard Bliss Brooke, Jim Rohn, John C. Maxwell, and Stephen Covey** are influential voices but provide motivational/leadership philosophy, not empirical evidence that most people succeed in MLM.
- **Supplement MLM vs retail comparisons** should rely on ingredient quality, third-party testing, price-per-dose, and EFSA/FDA-compliant health claims — not on the business model as evidence of superiority.

## Source labelling convention for downstream articles

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Fact** | Directly supported by primary legal/regulatory document, court decision, or official data. |
| **Guideline / Law** | Official legislation, regulation, or court decision. |
