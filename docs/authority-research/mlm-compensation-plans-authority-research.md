**Language:** Swedish

**Scope:** This document describes common MLM compensation plan structures, their legal implications, and how they relate to the pyramid-scheme boundary. It is a research input, not a finished article.

## 1. Topic

An **MLM compensation plan** is the system by which independent distributors earn income from product sales and from the sales or purchases of other distributors they recruit. The design of the plan determines whether the business operates primarily as a legitimate product-sales organisation or drifts toward an illegal pyramid scheme.

### Core definitions

| Term | Definition | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Compensation plan / marketing plan** | The commission and bonus structure that determines how distributors earn income. | Core legal and economic feature of any MLM. |
| **Retail sale / external sale** | Sale to a non-participant end user. | Strong indicator of legitimacy. |
| **Internal consumption** | Product purchased by distributors for personal use. | Legally contested; may be legitimate retail demand or inventory loading. |
| **Recruitment commission / override** | Commission earned from the sales or purchases of recruited distributors. | Legal if tied to genuine retail sales; problematic if tied mainly to recruitment. |
| **Rank / pin level** | Title achieved through volume, recruitment, or team size (e.g., Gold, Diamond, Executive). | Higher ranks typically unlock deeper commission levels. |
| **PV (Personal Volume)** | Sales/purchases credited to an individual distributor. | Often includes distributor's own purchases. |
| **GV (Group Volume)** | Combined volume of a distributor's downline. | Used to determine rank and bonuses. |
| **Inventory loading** | Requiring or strongly incentivising large product purchases to qualify for commissions or rank. | Key pyramid-scheme warning sign. |

## 3. Tier 0 original sources

### Regulatory / legal / government

- **FTC v. Amway (1979).**
  - **Type:** Administrative decision
  - **Importance:** Established that legal MLM compensation must be tied to retail sales; introduced the no-inventory-loading, buy-back, and 70%-rule safeguards.

- **FTC v. BurnLounge (9th Cir. 2015).**
  - **Type:** Federal appellate decision
  - **Importance:** Confirmed that rewards must be tied to ultimate-user sales, not merely to purchases by participants in the opportunity.

- **FTC / Herbalife settlement (2016).**
  - **Type:** Administrative settlement
  - **Importance:** Required Herbalife to restructure its compensation so that rewards were based on verifiable retail sales rather than recruitment; relevant for any plan analysis.

- **FTC Business Opportunity Rule (2012).**
  - **Type:** Federal rule
  - **Importance:** Requires disclosure of earnings, litigation, and refund policies for covered business opportunities.

### Industry / trade / educational sources

- **WFDSA Code of Ethics.**
  - **Type:** Industry self-regulation
  - **Importance:** States that compensation should be based on sales to ultimate users and prohibits inventory loading, but enforcement varies.

- **DSA-US Code of Ethics.**
  - **Type:** National trade-association code
  - **Importance:** Includes provisions on earnings claims and inventory practices.

- **Eric Worre. *Go Pro*.**
  - **Type:** MLM trainer/educator primary source
  - **Importance:** Describes industry norms around recruitment and commission structures from a participant perspective; not legal evidence.

### Peer-reviewed / critical analysis

- **Bosley, Stacey (2021).** MLM income-disclosure review.
  - **Type:** Peer-reviewed research
  - **Importance:** Provides aggregate context that most MLM participants earn little, regardless of plan type.

- **Groß & Keep (2025).** MLM/direct-selling research bias review.
  - **Type:** Peer-reviewed review
  - **Importance:** Warns that plan-related research may be industry-influenced.

- **Consumer Awareness Institute / Jon Taylor. *The Case (For and) Against MLM.***
  - **Type:** Independent research compilation
  - **Importance:** Critical analysis of MLM compensation economics, including the "recruitment-driven" nature of many plans.

## 5. Major research, reviews and regulatory decisions

### Common compensation-plan types

| Plan type | Structure | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Unilevel** | Distributors earn a percentage on sales of recruits several levels deep in a single organisation. | Common, relatively transparent; depth can vary (e.g., 5–10 levels). |
| **Binary** | Distributors build two "legs"; commissions depend on balanced volume between legs. | Creates pressure to recruit symmetrically; "spillover" is often misunderstood. |
| **Matrix** | Fixed-width, fixed-depth structure (e.g., 3×7 matrix); positions fill from top to bottom. | Can foster a sense of automatic growth that is often unrealistic. |
| **Breakaway / Stairstep** | Distributors can "break away" from their sponsor when they reach a certain rank; sponsor earns lower override on breakaway volume. | Common in older, larger MLMs; creates complex rank dynamics. |
| **Hybrid** | Combinations of the above. | Most real-world plans are hybrid. |

### Key commission components

- **Retail profit / customer commission:** Markup or commission on sales to retail customers.
- **Wholesale commission:** Difference between distributor price and suggested retail price.
- **Override / team commission:** Percentage of downline sales volume.
- **Rank advancement bonuses:** One-time or recurring bonuses for reaching ranks.
- **Leadership pool:** Share of company-wide volume allocated to top ranks.
- **Fast-start bonuses:** Bonuses for recruiting new distributors who place initial orders.

Legally, the critical issue is what proportion of total compensation is traceable to genuine retail sales versus recruitment or participant purchases.

### Landmark regulatory decisions

- **FTC v. Amway (1979)** — compensation tied to retail sales + inventory safeguards = not pyramid.
- **FTC v. BurnLounge (2015)** — compensation tied to purchases by participants in the opportunity = pyramid.
- **FTC v. Herbalife (2016)** — restructuring required to verify retail sales; changed reward calculations.
- **FTC v. Vemma (2015)** — focused on recruitment-driven compensation and rank qualification tied to recruitment.

### Research findings

- **Bosley (2021):** Most MLM participants earn little or nothing across plan types.
- **FTC (2024):** Income-disclosure data often obscure the fact that most participants earn little.
- **Groß & Keep (2025):** Much plan-related literature is industry-tied.

## 7. Established scientific / regulatory consensus

1. **Compensation must derive primarily from retail sales to ultimate users for an MLM to be legally sound.** This is the core lesson of FTC v. Amway, BurnLounge, and Herbalife.
2. **No compensation-plan type is automatically legal or illegal.** Legality depends on how the plan operates in practice.
3. **Inventory loading, large up-front purchases, and rank qualifications tied mainly to recruitment are warning signs of pyramid risk.**
4. **Plan complexity can obscure income sources.** Simple, transparent disclosures help consumers assess opportunity risk.
5. **Most MLM participants earn little or no net profit across all major plan types.** Plan design affects distribution but does not change the aggregate finding.
6. **Internal consumption by distributors is legally contested.** Some jurisdictions accept it as legitimate retail demand; regulators scrutinise it as potential inventory loading.
7. **Rank and title systems create motivational and social pressure but do not guarantee income.** Reaching a high rank usually requires building a large organisation.
8. **Fast-start and recruitment bonuses increase pyramid risk** when they reward recruitment itself rather than subsequent retail sales.
9. **WFDSA/DSA codes of ethics discourage inventory loading and misleading income claims,** but enforcement is self-regulatory and not a substitute for government oversight.
10. **Consumers evaluating an MLM opportunity should ask:** Who are the ultimate users? What percentage of compensation comes from retail sales? Is there a reasonable buy-back policy? What do independent income disclosures show?

## 8. Areas of controversy

1. **Internal consumption as legitimate retail demand.** Industry advocates argue distributor self-use is a valid sale; critics argue it inflates volume and masks recruitment-driven economics.
2. **Percentage of retail sales required for legality.** No bright-line statutory percentage exists; regulators and courts apply totality-of-circumstances tests.
3. **Binary plan spillover and balance requirements.** Critics argue binaries encourage unhealthy recruitment pressure; defenders say they encourage teamwork.
4. **Auto-ship / monthly personal-volume requirements.** Monthly purchase requirements to remain active or qualify for commissions may amount to inventory loading.
5. **Rank qualification through recruitment rather than retail sales.** Plans that require recruits to advance create strong pyramid incentives.
6. **Disclosure of true income sources.** Income-disclosure statements rarely separate retail commissions from recruitment overrides.
7. **Buy-back policy adequacy.** Industry codes require buy-back policies; critics argue restrictions and partial credits weaken protection.
8. **Digital/social-media compensation tools.** Modern plans use apps and social tracking; regulators lag in assessing these mechanisms.

## 9. Common misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction / nuance |
|---|---|
| "Binary plans are pyramid schemes; unilevel plans are not." | No plan type is automatically legal or illegal; the economics matter. |
| "A plan that pays on many levels is by definition a pyramid." | Multi-level payout is legal if tied to genuine retail sales. |
| "If I can buy products for personal use, the plan is legal." | Personal-use purchases alone do not make a plan legitimate; compensation must derive from ultimate-user sales. |
| "High rank means high income." | Rank reflects organisational volume, not necessarily net profit after expenses. |
| "Spillover in a binary plan guarantees income." | Spillover depends on recruitment activity above you; it is not automatic. |
| "Complex plans are more profitable." | Complexity often obscures the fact that most participants earn little. |
| "No inventory-loading requirement means the plan is safe." | Absence of one red flag is good but not sufficient; consider overall economics and disclosure. |
| "All compensation plans are reviewed by regulators." | Most plans are not pre-approved; enforcement is usually reactive. |

## Factual consistency notes

- **No compensation-plan type is inherently legal or illegal.** Legality depends on the source of compensation.
- **Avoid stating binary, matrix, or unilevel plans are automatically pyramid schemes.** They can be operated legally or illegally.
- **Internal consumption is controversial, not settled law.** Present both industry and regulatory perspectives.
- **Rank titles do not indicate income.** Always distinguish organisational volume from net profit.
- **Spillover and "done-for-you" growth claims are typically misleading.** Explain the mechanics honestly.
- **Use FTC and Konsumentverket terminology consistently** (ultimate user, inventory loading, retail customer).
