## 1. Topic

An **income disclosure statement (IDS)** is a document published by an MLM or direct-selling company that provides information about the earnings of its independent distributors. Regulators, consumer advocates, and critical researchers use IDS data to assess the typical income participants can expect. The FTC and academic researchers have found that many IDS are misleading or difficult to interpret.

### Core definitions

| Term | Definition | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Income Disclosure Statement (IDS)** | Company document summarising distributor earnings over a period. | Often required by DSA codes and U.S. law; practices vary globally. |
| **Earnings claim** | Any statement about income, lifestyle, or financial success in connection with a business opportunity. | Regulated under FTC Act and Swedish Marketing Act. |
| **Median / mean income** | Central-tendency measures of distributor earnings. | Mean is easily skewed by a few high earners; median is more typical. |
| **Percentile rank** | Shows what percentage of distributors earn above/below a threshold (e.g., top 1%). | Useful for understanding income distribution. |
| **Active distributor** | A distributor who meets minimum activity criteria during the reporting period. | Definitions vary; inactive distributors may be excluded, inflating average figures. |
| **Gross income vs net income** | Gross income does not subtract expenses such as product purchases, shipping, travel, or marketing. | Most IDS report gross figures. |
| **Zero-earner / non-earner** | Distributors who earned no commission during the period. | Often omitted from "average income" calculations in misleading disclosures. |

## 3. Tier 0 original sources

### Regulatory / government

- **FTC Consumer Alert (September 4, 2024).** *What are multi-level marketing (MLM) disclosure statements really telling you?*
  - **Type:** Original government publication
  - **Importance:** Most recent and authoritative analysis of IDS misleading tactics.
  - **Key findings:** Most participants made $1,000 or less per year in reviewed MLMs; in at least 17 MLMs most made nothing; companies use emphasised top earners, averages, and confusing presentation.

- **FTC Business Opportunity Rule (2012).**
  - **Type:** Federal rule
  - **Importance:** Requires disclosure of earnings claims, litigation history, and refund terms for covered business opportunities.

- **FTC v. Herbalife (2016 consent order).**
  - **Type:** Administrative settlement
  - **Importance:** Required Herbalife to provide clear, honest, and verified disclosures of typical distributor income.

- **FTC Guides Concerning the Use of Endorsements and Testimonials in Advertising (2009, updated).**
  - **Type:** FTC guidance
  - **Importance:** Governs income testimonials and atypical-result disclosures.

- **Directive 2005/29/EC (UCPD).**
  - **Type:** EU legislation
  - **Importance:** Prohibits misleading commercial practices, including misleading income claims and omissions.

- **Marknadsföringslagen (2008:486).**
  - **Type:** Swedish legislation
  - **Importance:** Prohibits misleading marketing, including income claims by Swedish direct sellers and MLMs.

  - **Type:** Peer-reviewed research
  - **Importance:** Estimated ~25% of U.S. MLM sellers make a profit; reviews how averages are calculated.

## 5. Major research, reviews and regulatory decisions

### FTC Consumer Alert (2024)

The FTC's September 2024 Consumer Alert analysed income disclosures from dozens of MLMs and identified common misleading tactics:

- **Top-earner emphasis:** Highlighting a few high earners makes the opportunity look more lucrative than it is for the median participant.
- **Averaging out zero earners:** Reporting mean income among "active" distributors while excluding those who earned nothing inflates the apparent average.
- **Omitting expenses:** Disclosure of gross income without business expenses overstates actual profit.
- **Confusing presentation:** Using percentages without denominators, mixing annual and monthly figures, or burying important data in footnotes.
- **Ignoring churn:** High distributor turnover means many people leave before earning anything; IDS often fail to account for this.

### FTC Business Opportunity Rule

- Requires a one-page disclosure document for covered business opportunities.
- Must include earnings claims (if made), litigation history, and refund/cancellation policy.
- Does not cover all MLMs because some compensation structures fall outside the rule's definition, but the FTC Act still prohibits deceptive claims.

### FTC v. Herbalife (2016)

- Herbalife agreed to hire an independent compliance auditor.
- Required to provide clear, honest, and verified income disclosures.
- Demonstrated that regulators view inadequate disclosure as a core consumer-protection problem.

### Peer-reviewed research

- **Bosley (2021):** Found that headline income averages in MLM disclosures are often misleading because they exclude inactive and zero-earning participants.
- **Groß & Keep (2025):** Found that industry-tied research systematically downplays risks, including low earnings.

## 7. Established scientific / regulatory consensus

1. **Income disclosure statements are a key transparency tool but often present an overly optimistic picture of MLM earnings.**
2. **Most participants in reviewed MLMs earn little or no gross income, and even fewer earn a net profit after expenses.**
3. **Mean averages are misleading when income is highly skewed; median income and percentile breakdowns are more informative.**
4. **Disclosures must not omit zero earners, understate expenses, or highlight atypical top earners without clear qualification.**
5. **Under U.S. law, any earnings claim must be truthful, non-deceptive, and substantiated.** The FTC Act applies even where the Business Opportunity Rule does not.
6. **Under EU and Swedish law, income claims in marketing are subject to the prohibition on misleading commercial practices.**
7. **High distributor churn means that many participants are not represented in "active distributor" statistics.**
8. **Industry self-regulation (DSA codes) encourages IDS publication but does not eliminate misleading practices.**
9. **Independent academic analysis confirms that headline IDS figures can substantially overstate typical outcomes.**
10. **Consumers should read IDS for median income, zero-earner rate, expense disclaimers, and the definition of "active" distributor before making decisions.**

---

## 8. Areas of controversy

1. **Should IDS be legally mandatory for all MLMs?** FTC and consumer advocates generally support stronger requirements; industry prefers self-regulation.
2. **How should expenses be disclosed?** Most companies omit expenses; there is no consensus on standardised expense reporting.
3. **What is the correct denominator?** Should zero earners, inactive participants, and churned distributors be included?
4. **How should "active" be defined?** Varying definitions make cross-company comparisons difficult.
5. **Are income testimonials inherently misleading?** Testimonials can be truthful for one person but unrepresentative; regulators require atypical-result disclaimers.
6. **Should multi-year disclosures be required?** One-year snapshots may not reflect volatility and churn.
7. **Cross-border harmonisation.** U.S. IDS practices differ from EU/Swedish marketing-law requirements; global companies may present inconsistent disclosures.
8. **Can IDS be simplified for consumers without losing accuracy?** Balancing completeness and readability is an ongoing challenge.

## 9. Common misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction / nuance |
|---|---|
| "The average income in the disclosure is what I will earn." | Mean averages are skewed by top earners; median or percentile figures are more typical. |
| "Income disclosed is take-home pay." | Most IDS report gross income before business expenses and taxes. |
| "If a company publishes an IDS, it must be honest." | Disclosure is required or encouraged, but content can still be misleading. |
| "Top earners prove the model works." | A small percentage earning significant income does not indicate typical results. |
| "No earnings data means the company is hiding something." | Absence of an IDS is a red flag, but presence does not guarantee transparency. |
| "Inactive distributors should not be counted." | Excluding them inflates averages; the full participant pool is more representative. |
| "Swedish MLMs are not bound by U.S. disclosure rules." | True, but Swedish/EU marketing law still prohibits misleading income claims. |
| "Income testimonials on social media are not regulated." | Testimonials used in marketing are generally covered by marketing and advertising law. |

## Factual consistency notes

- **Always distinguish gross from net income.** Most IDS are gross.
- **Mean averages are not typical.** Use median and percentiles where possible.
- **Do not use IDS figures to predict individual outcomes.** Disclosures are historical aggregates.
- **"Active" definitions vary.** Always note that exclusions can inflate apparent averages.
- **Expense omission is a major limitation.** Consumers should consider product purchases, shipping, travel, marketing, and time costs.
- **FTC 2024 language should be quoted carefully.** It reviewed "dozens of MLMs" and identified specific misleading tactics.
